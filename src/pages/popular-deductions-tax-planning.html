We Are Here To Help You

The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) provides guidelines on a variety of popular tax deductions and credits to help taxpayers reduce their taxable income and potentially increase their tax refunds. Here is an overview of some commonly utilized deductions and credits, along with key IRS guideline

Standard Deductions

Guidelines: The standard deduction is a fixed dollar amount that reduces the income on which you are taxed. The IRS adjusts these amounts annually.

Recent Update: The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) nearly doubled the standard deduction, making it more attractive for many taxpayers.

Itemized Deductions

Guidelines: Taxpayers can choose to itemize deductions instead of taking the standard deduction. Eligible expenses include medical expenses, state and local taxes, mortgage interest, and charitable contributions.

Documentation: Keep detailed records and receipts to support itemized deductions. Certain limits and restrictions may apply.

Child Tax Credit

Guidelines: The Child Tax Credit provides a credit of up to $2,000 per qualifying child. Income limits and other eligibility criteria apply.

Refundable Portion: Up to $1,400 of the credit per child may be refundable.

Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

Guidelines: The EITC is a credit for low to moderate-income individuals and families. The amount of the credit depends on income, filing status, and the number of qualifying children.

Income Limits: Income limits are adjusted annually. It is crucial to stay updated on the current limits.

Education Credits

Guidelines: The American Opportunity Credit and the Lifetime Learning Credit provide tax credits for qualifying education expenses. Eligibility criteria include enrollment status and income limits.

Form 1098-T: Educational institutions provide Form 1098-T to help taxpayers claim these credits.

Mortgage Interest Deduction

Guidelines: Taxpayers can deduct interest paid on qualified mortgage loans. There are limits on the amount of mortgage debt eligible for the deduction.

Home Equity Debt: TCJA eliminated the deduction for interest on home equity debt that is not used to buy, build, or substantially improve the home.

Health Savings Account (HSA) Deduction

Guidelines: Contributions to HSAs are tax-deductible, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free.

Contribution Limits: The IRS sets annual contribution limits for HSAs.

Retirement Savings Contributions Credit (Saver's Credit)

Guidelines: The Saver's Credit provides a credit for eligible contributions to retirement savings accounts, such as 401(k)s and IRAs.

Income Limits: Income limits apply, and the credit percentage varies based on filing status and income.

State and Local Tax (SALT) Deduction

Guidelines: Taxpayers can deduct state and local income taxes or sales taxes, and property taxes paid during the tax year.

Limitations: TCJA imposed a $10,000 cap on the total deduction for state and local property, income, and sales taxes.
